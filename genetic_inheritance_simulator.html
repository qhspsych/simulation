The Coin Flip of Inheritance

Every chromosome you carry came from a coin flip. Your parents each had two copies per chromosome — one from their mother, one from their father — and passed one to you at random. This simulator lets you watch that process unfold.

Each of your 23 chromosome pairs arrived via a coin flip: heads = one grandparent's copy, tails = the other's. Your mother and father each ran 23 independent flips. The result, combined, is you. Hit Flip Chromosomes to simulate a new baby.

Crossing over (recombination): before a chromosome is selected, the two copies splice segments together — e.g., the first third from grandma, the rest from grandpa. This happens ~2 times per chromosome, tripling the effective number of coin flips from 46 to 138. The number of possible children jumps from 246 (~70 trillion) to 2138 (~350 duodecillion).
The binomial distribution describes how many chromosomes a child inherits from any one grandparent. With 23 independent coin flips, the probability of extreme outcomes (all from one grandparent, none from the other) is vanishingly small. Most children land near the middle.

Probability distribution — chromosomes from one grandparent (out of 23)

Why the middle dominates: There is only one way to get all 23 from grandma (every flip must come up heads). But to get 12 from grandma and 11 from grandpa, there are 1,352,078 different orderings. Ordinary outcomes are ordinary because there are so many paths to them. Extreme outcomes are extreme because there is barely one.
Two siblings each undergo their own independent 46 coin flips. In principle they could share anywhere from 0 to all 46 chromosomes — but the binomial distribution pulls both toward the middle. Add siblings and watch how much they share.
Based on the analogy from Math With Bad Drawings by Ben Orlin. Each chromosome represents a simplified single-unit inheritance event; real genetics involves crossing over, recombination, and much greater complexity.
